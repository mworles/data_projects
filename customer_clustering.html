Customer Segmentation Project

In this project the goal was to identify segments of customers who purchased wine in response email offers for wine discounts.

In this type of project, I don't know ahead of time whether any particular customer segments actually exist. This is an example of an "unsupervised learning" problem. There is no outcome or "target" in the data to predict. Instead, I will use algorithms designed to identify the unknown structure in the data, if any exists.

Why use customer segmentation?


In this use case (customers ordering wine in response to an email), why would I want to identify customer segments? I can think of two good reasons:

  • Focused marketing - If a segment exhibits certain purchasing tendencies or preferences, future offers can be personlized to fit those tendencies
  • Steer growth - If some segments produce greater yields than others, future offers or acquisition of customers can focus on appealing to these segments

Clustering algorithm: K-Means Clustering

With K-Means clustering the goal is essentially to create groups of customers so that the customers in each group are similar to each other, and dissimilar from customers in other groups. I don't know ahead of time which "true" customer segments exist, how many segments exist, what types of customers make up each segment. This algorithm will look for patterns in the purchase matrix above to group "like" customers together.

With K-means clustering, I have to tell the algorithm the number of clusters to form. Because I don't know the best number for this data, I'll test different numbers of clusters and use a scoring method to see how well each solution groups the data. The silhouette score summarizes how well each data point "fits" its assigned cluster and how poorly it fits into other clusters. The scores range from -1 to 1, with scores closer to 1 indicative of a better clustering solution.

In [17]:
data = dfpv.values
clusters=range(2,11)
scores = []

for n in clusters:
    kmod = KMeans(n_clusters=n, random_state=10)
    cluster_labels = kmod.fit_predict(data)
    score = silhouette_score(data, cluster_labels)
    scores.append(score)

Having obtained the scores for solutions with 2-11 clusters, I plot the scores to see which solution has the highest score.

Summary and Recommendations

The customers fall into 5 segments that can be characterized by the varietals they ordered. In order of size, the segments are:

  • Mixed (32%) - A mix of all varietals, most often Champagne and Cabernet Sauvignon
  • Mixed, no Champagne (29%) - A mix of a few varietals, but no Champagne, Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, and rarely Cab
  • Mixed, Heavy Champagne (16%) - A mix of all varietals but especially Champagne
  • Pinot Noir (16%) - Pinot Noir and very rarely anything else
  • Sparkling (7%) - Only varietals that come in sparkling form

The champagne-ordering segments return the most orders per customer, led by the Mixed, Heavy Champagne segment (3.5 orders).
The non-Champagne segments return the fewest orders per customer. The Mixed, no Champagne segment had the lowest order rate (2.2 orders/customer), and is also the second-largest segment.

Based on this analysis, I recommend that the company personalize future offers to fit each segment's purchasing tendencies, as follows:

  • Sparkling wine segment - Only sparkling wines
  • Pinot Noir segment - Only Pinot Noir
  • Mixed, no Champagne segment - A mix of varietals not including Champagne, Chardonnay, or Pinot Noir
  • Mixed and Mixed, Heavy Champagne segments - A mix of varietals with an emphasis on Champagne

I also recommend that the company focus customer acquisition efforts on new customers that buy champagne, since segments with these customers tend to produce the greatest return per customer.
